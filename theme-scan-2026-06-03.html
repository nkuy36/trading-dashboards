02 Section 122 Tariff Cliff / Trade Policy Succession HIGH EARLY
CAT  DE  URI  NUE  LMT  RTX  NOC
REXR  PLD  Mexico/Vietnam EMs
WMT  TGT  AMZN  AAPL supply chain  NKE
4–6 weeks; tariff decisions likely before July 24
Evidence
US Court of International Trade struck down Section 122 tariffs on May 7, 2026 (2-1 ruling), but CAFC administrative stay keeps them in effect. Tariffs still being collected. [Source]
Section 122 surcharge expires July 24, 2026. Administration has initiated wide-ranging Section 301 investigations intended to conclude before the July 24 expiry. [Source]
Markets pricing July 24 as a relief event. Section 301 successor could produce permanent, differentiated tariffs. Yale Budget Lab estimates all 2026 tariffs increase prices ~0.6% short-run. [Source]
The consensus trade on tariffs is "relief by July 24 expiry" — but the real risk is the Section 301 successor investigations quietly advancing. Section 301 tariffs are permanent, require no congressional action, and can be differentiated by country and product. The market is misreading this as a sunset when it may be a transition to a more permanent regime. Import-heavy retailers and consumer electronics supply chains are the most exposed to a negative surprise. Domestic manufacturers are the asymmetric longs.

04 Section 122 Tariff Expiry / Section 301 Succession Decision HIGH
July 24, 2026
~Late June 2026
WMT  TGT  AMZN  AAPL  NKE vs. CAT  DE  NUE
Administration announces Section 301 successor tariffs — permanent, elevated, differentiated by country. Import-heavy consumer stocks have rallied on expiry expectations and would reprice sharply negative on this news.
The Section 122 tariff expiry on July 24 is being widely read as bullish for importers. This ignores the active Section 301 investigation program designed to produce a permanent successor regime before the deadline. Section 301 tariffs are permanent, require no congressional renewal, and can be applied asymmetrically by country and product. The Court of International Trade's May 7 ruling against Section 122 (currently stayed) adds legal urgency for the administration to lock in a 301 framework. Any late-June announcement of 301 tariff schedules would be sharply negative for the consumer sector.
